What is vibe coding?

You describe; the AI builds; you steer.

Building software by directing an AI in plain language instead of typing every line yourself.

  • You stay the architect — decisions, taste, and review are still yours.
  • The AI does the typing, the boilerplate, the busywork.
  • Not "ignore the code and hope" — we keep a hand on the wheel.
Define it simply. Emphasize: vibe coding ≠ specs-to-code-and-ignore-the-code. The code is still the battleground. You're the architect; AI is the very fast junior who never sleeps. Tonight shows the disciplined version.

What is an LLM?

A prediction engine that thinks in text.

  • A model trained to predict the next piece of text — scaled up until it can reason, code, and explain.
  • It "thinks" in the words you give it — so what you put in front of it matters enormously.
  • It has no memory between sessions — every fresh chat starts clean.
Keep it concrete for data folks: it's a probabilistic model — same family as the prediction stuff you already know, just enormous. Two properties drive everything tonight: (1) it reasons in the text you give it (context = its working memory), (2) it forgets between sessions. Those two facts shape the whole workflow.

Smart zone vs dumb zone

The one idea that makes AI coding work.

Smart zone

Fresh, focused context. Small task. The model does its best work here.

Dumb zone

Context stuffed full (~100k+ tokens). Attention strained. It starts making silly mistakes.

  • Keep tasks small so the work fits in the smart zone.
  • Clear and restart, don't "compact." The model is the guy from Memento — a fresh start is its smartest start.
  • Humans own alignment & planning; agents do implementation.
This is the spine. Credit: Dex Horthy / Human Layer, popularized by Matt Pocock. As context fills, quality drops — roughly past ~100k tokens. So we size tasks small and start fresh often. I'll show the token counter live. "Clear vs compact": compacting keeps sediment; clearing returns to the model's sharpest state. Everything that follows exists to keep us in the green zone.

/grill-with-docs

Step 1 · ALIGN  Get on the same page before any code.

The AI interviews you — one question at a time — until you both picture the same app.

  • It walks the decision tree, surfaces what you hadn't thought of, and recommends an answer each time — you accept or redirect.
  • It captures the shared words (a glossary) and the big decisions, so they stick.
How to say it
"The #1 reason AI coding goes sideways is you and the AI wanting different things. This fixes that — it grills you until you're aligned. It's the most important step; everything after is only as good as this."
Matt Pocock's favorite skill — four sentences that change everything. The goal isn't a document, it's a SHARED DESIGN CONCEPT (Fred Brooks): you and the AI on the same wavelength. It asks relentlessly, recommends an answer, you steer. For beginners: it's a relentless, helpful teammate that asks the questions you forgot to. We'll run it live against our spec sheet. (Matt: "I needed to be on the same wavelength as my agent.")

/to-prd

Step 2 · DESTINATION  Write down where we're going.

Turns the conversation into a one-page plan — the destination document.

  • Problem · solution · user stories · what's in & out of scope · definition of "done."
  • You don't agonize over it — you just confirm the AI captured what you agreed. (AI is great at summarizing.)
How to say it
"Now we capture what we agreed on as a one-page destination — our north star. We skim it to check the AI got the gist; we don't re-read every word. It also tells us when we're done."
Matt: the "destination document." Because AI is great at summarizing, we trust it to summarize our alignment and we just skim. PRD = Product Requirements Document. The most useful parts for beginners: the user stories ("as a vendor, I want to scan a badge so that…") and the OUT-OF-SCOPE list (that's how you avoid scope creep and know when to stop). We'll generate it live straight from the grill.

/to-issues

Step 3 · SLICE  Cut it into bite-sized, end-to-end pieces.

Breaks the plan into small vertical slices — each a complete thread from button to result.

  • "Tracer bullets": every slice does something you can see and test.
  • Not "all the plumbing, then all the buttons" — that way you get no feedback until the end.
How to say it
"We chop the work into thin slices, each one a full thread you can actually run. So we see something working after every slice — and it keeps each task small enough to stay in the smart zone."
Matt's tracer-bullets idea (from The Pragmatic Programmer): if you fire in the dark you can't see where your shots land — tracer rounds glow so you get feedback. AI loves to build HORIZONTALLY (all one layer at a time), but then nothing works until the very end. We force VERTICAL slices: thin, complete threads through the whole stack. Each becomes a small, independently-grabbable task — and you can even run several in parallel. We'll slice our app live and pick the first one to build.

/tdd

Step 4 · BUILD & TEST  Prove the logic works — test first.

Write a tiny failing test that describes the behavior, then just enough code to pass. Repeat.

  • Red → Green → Refactor, one behavior at a time.
  • The test is a clear target for the AI — and it's much harder for it to "cheat."
How to say it
"Where there's real logic, we write the test first — a little check that fails — then make it pass. The code actually works, it keeps working, and the AI has a bullseye to aim at."
Matt: TDD is essential for getting the most out of agents — it adds good tests AND stops the AI from faking it (write code, then a test that just rubber-stamps it). Your feedback loops are the CEILING on AI quality. We already used this on the one piece of real logic in our app — reading a name+email off a QR code (5 tests, all green). We can pop those open. Don't TDD everything (not the UI) — use it where there's logic worth trusting.

How we actually got here — setup

The real steps — not just "a handful of prompts."

1
Started from a real client meeting. The conference committee, recorded. We turned that conversation into a one-page spec — plain English, no code. Align before you build.
2
Asked the AI to pick the right tool: "what's the best way to scan QR codes in a browser today?" It flagged the most popular library (html5-qrcode) as abandoned — no release since 2023, known camera bugs — so we chose a maintained one.
3
That same research surfaced an Apple bug: the camera won't run in an installed home-screen app. Decision: keep it a plain URL, not an installed app — written down as a one-paragraph ADR.
4
Let the AI scaffold the shell: "set up a small Vite + React app with the QR library." It installed everything and handed back a running app — in minutes.
This is the real story — reference the actual conversation if anyone asks. We didn't just "prompt an app into being." We started from a recorded client meeting, asked the AI to research the QR library (it saved us from an abandoned one AND surfaced the iOS camera bug), wrote that decision down as an ADR, then let it scaffold the shell. The value was in the research + the one decision — not the typing.

How we got here — making it solid

Tests, sample data, and chasing one real snag.

5
Test-drove the one piece of real logic — reading a name + email off a badge — test-first. Five tiny cycles: encode a vCard → parse one back → tolerate real-world quirks (CRLF, extra fields) → ignore junk → round-trip. All green.
6
Made 3 sample badges with a one-screen script that reuses that same tested code — so the badges and the scanner can't drift apart.
7
Gave the AI a browser (Playwright) to open the app and QA it like a user.
8
Tested on a real iPhone over a public link — first try: a 403. The dev server blocks unknown web addresses for security; the error told us the exact fix; we added one line. Camera confirmed on iOS + Android. Snag → the error tells you the fix → done.

Grand total: a conversation, a few prompts, two decisions, one script — about an hour, mostly the AI typing.

The making-it-solid half. TDD on the one bit of real logic (reading a contact off a QR) — 5 small cycles, walk one if they're curious. Sample badges reuse the same tested code. Playwright gives the AI eyes. Then the real test: on a phone we hit a 403, the error named the fix, one line.
